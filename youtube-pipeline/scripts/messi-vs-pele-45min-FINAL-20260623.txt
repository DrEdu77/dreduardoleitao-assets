[CHAPTER 7: THE SCIENCE OF THE IMPOSSIBLE — HOW IS MESSI STILL DOING THIS AT 38? — 39:30]

Sports scientists have been studying Messi's physical preservation for three years. And the data is genuinely difficult to explain.

In 2023, researchers at the Barcelona Institute of High Performance Sport published a study tracking Messi's physical metrics across his career from 2005 to 2023. Their conclusions were, in the words of the lead researcher Dr. Carles Padullés, "consistent with what we would expect from a player between 27 and 32 years old."

Messi was 35 when the study was conducted.

How?

The answer is multifactorial — meaning there isn't one reason. There are several.

First: his movement style.

Throughout his career, Messi has been almost uniquely efficient in how he uses his body. Unlike strikers who rely on pace and explosive power — which degrades rapidly after 30 — Messi's game is built on acceleration over short distances, spatial intelligence, and precision passing. These are cognitive and neurological assets, not purely physical ones. And they age far more slowly than sprint speed.

By 2022, Messi's top speed had dropped from the 32 km/h he recorded in his Barcelona prime to approximately 28 km/h. His peak acceleration time — the time it takes to reach full speed from a standing position — had declined by approximately 12%. But his passing accuracy, his position selection, and his decision-making speed had actually improved.

He is not faster. He is smarter. And in football at the highest level, smart outperforms fast by a significant margin after 32.

Second: recovery and nutrition.

Messi's dietary regimen since approximately 2015 — supervised by nutritionist Giuliano Poser — eliminated most processed foods, refined sugars, and heavy proteins, replacing them with a diet dominated by water, olive oil, whole grains, and lean protein. The motivation, he has said in interviews, was not philosophical but pragmatic: he had noticed that his recovery time between matches was increasing, and he wanted to find a way to reduce it.

Over the following decade, he essentially redesigned his body's recovery architecture. His resting heart rate — typically between 48 and 52 beats per minute — remains in the range of an elite endurance athlete fifteen years his junior.

Third: psychology.

This is the factor that science struggles most to quantify. But every coach who has worked with Messi at both club and national team level has made the same observation in some form: he does not experience competitive pressure the way most players do. Under conditions that produce anxiety, adrenaline, cortisol spikes, and the physical degradation that comes with them — Messi appears to slow down internally.

His heart rate during penalties, documented in biometric studies conducted with Inter Miami in 2024, averages 82 beats per minute. Most elite athletes reach 140 or higher in equivalent conditions. His hands are steady. His breathing is controlled.

When the 2026 World Cup was announced, Messi said in an interview with L'Equipe — translated from the original French — "I'm not thinking about records. I'm thinking about winning. When the record comes, I'll know what it means. Not before."

He was right. It came. He knew.

And what it meant, ultimately, was everything.
